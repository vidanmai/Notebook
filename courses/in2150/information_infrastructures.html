Information Infrastructures

Work-oriented infrastructures

Hanseth and Lundberg (2001)

This derm draw our attention to the fact that such infrastructures are developed to support specific work tasks and practices as opposed to the simple and universal services provided by traditional infrastructures.

Work oriented infrastructures means infrastructures that support a field of work in an organization.

Hospital case - radiological work practices

  • Hanseth and Ludberg (2001)
  • Radiology department in a hospital
    • Implementation and use of two systems:
    - PACS: Picture Archiving and Communication systems
    - RIS: Radiology Information System
  • From the perspective of 'work': the work practices for performing an x-ray examination and writing the report:
    - The workflow: who does what and when, in which sequence
    - The technical infrastructure: Which artefacts are used (IT and others)
    - How does the change in the infrastructure affect work?
  • They show the tight interdependencies between social (work) and technical (infrastructure)

The traditional x-ray infrastructure

It is based on physical artifacts such as referral, result-report and image

These artifacts play many roles:

  • Artefacts contain and take information around
  • The artefacts coordinate the work
  • Artefacts are inderdependent - depend on each other, e.g. shelves + paper, images + physical films

Task Chain and network

  • A patient trajectory cuts across many departments (clinical + radiology)
  • A clinical department is related to many service departments (labs, examination rooms, etc.)
  • A service department is related to many clinical departments (users)
  • Each department has many patients, each with his own trajectory

The result is: a large sociotechnical network where many artefacts are used for coordination and for supporting the information 'flow'.

Work-oriented infrastructures

The infrastructure is made of 'sub networks'
Radiology department + secretaries + clinicians + external units

Changes may take place in the four different 'sub-networks' and in their relations (gateways)

  • E.g. PACS expanded the digital infrastructure
  • E.g. PACS accessible by clinicians
  • E.g. gateways between digital/paper-base (scanners, printers)

Making gradual changes in the infrastructure with attention to how work practices are affected.

Standardization across multiple local settings

Based on Ellingsn and Monteiro (2006). We use this article for the case about Standardization (not the theory about sociotechnical integration).

The case:

  • Hospitals
  • Implementation of a new modular solution for the hospita core systems
  • One module for all the laboratories - replacing different lab systems
  • Goal: to improve productivity (better tracking of orders to the service/lab units)
  • Problem: different work practices in Clinical Chemistry lab and Microbiology lab - they work differently with analyses, results, interpretations.
  • Microbiology lab refuses to change system
Too much information for users
"The HealthSys system has both strengths and weaknesses by being so integrated with other laboratories... but for us it is not helpful... just consider that you have to scroll the listbox (the list of analysis codes). This also means that there are too many available options for the physicians orderings a test"

"mixing everything together in a big chunk on the wroking list has caused a problem for several departments because they want Microbiology results separated from the mass produced blood results"

The new lab system

Is it a standardization of communication practices? Or is it a standardization of sequence of tasks, or terminology?

Standardization as order making across contexts

Standardization creates tension across contexts. It can lead to order and disorder.

The standardization process imposes an order of uniformity. Across settings it can produce 'disorder' or unintended consequences. E.g. access to results; available options for physicians when ordering tests; "hidden" results.

So what do we standardize? The vendor based the design of system on most productive laboratories. It makes Microbiology invisible/problematic.

Summing up

Work oriented infrastructures
  • It's a perspective to describe infrastructures in relation to specific work settings
  • Infrastructures as made of 'sub-networks'
  • Changes can take place at different 'levels' and with consequences within the same sub-network or with consequences for other sub-networks.
Standardization across contexts
  • Infrastructures are shared resources.
  • Used by different users groups in different contexts
  • This creates interdependencies and unintended consequences when changes are driven by 'local' needs
  • There are many 'locals'

The bootstrap problem

Based on Hanseth, O., & Lyytinen, K. (2010). Design Theory for dynamic complexity in information infrastructures: the case of building internet
The bootstrap problem: how to persuade users to adopt while the user community is non-existent or small.
How to initiate change in infrastructures.

The technology designers need to persuade the first users to adopt a novel technology and deal with the initial uncertainties that com with it.

  1. Design initially for usefulness
  2. Draw upon the existing installed base
  3. Expand the installed base by persuasive tactics

It is a start-up problem

The adaptability problem

The adaptability problem: how to accomodate unforeseen and diverse demands and produce designs that can cope technically and cocially with the increasing varying needs?

  • Tensions between 'global' needs and local use, between the many locals.
  • Tensions between addressing immediate needs and long-term aims.

Technology designers need to addres the unforeseen and diverse demands and produce designs that cope with the increasingly varying needs.

  1. Make each IT capability simple
  2. Modularize the II by building separately its principal functions and sub-infrastructures using layering and gateways

Design of information infrastructures

Systems

  • IT-capabilities (a specific function for use)
  • Applications (collection of capabilities)
  • Platforms (bigger collection of applications and capabilities)
  • Information infrastructures

Information infrastructure

Consists of an installed base. It is sosio-technical, recursive and standards are core elements in II's. Installed base:

  • Open
  • Shared
  • Heterogenous
  • Evolving

Hanseth and Lyytinen (2010)

Tackling how to handle dynamic complexity in information infrastructures.

Can't be controlled and designed "top down" -> distributed control.

Article attempts to give design principles related to two core issues:

  • Bootstrapping problem
  • Adaptability problem

Bootstrapping

  • "To promote or develop by initiative and effort with little to no assistance"
  • "The process of making a tool by means of the tool itsef"

Simply explained as a strategy for initiating a process. It's both a tecnical and a social phenomena.

For a "new" information infrastructure to have value, it first needs users. The users have an important value in themselves. More users increase the credibility, so more will arrive.
Design principles for the II bootstrap problem:

  1. Design initially for usefulness
  2. Draw upon the existing installed base
  3. Expand installed base by persuasive tactics

Adaptability problem

The system is growing and is continously receiving new users and organizations in new contexts. The issue is then to support further implementations, development and use of system.

Path dependencies and lock-ins

As an II is evolving will earlier choices affect possible choices later. Therefore we call it path dependent.

Some choices can lead to lock-ins, where further possibilties for changees are limited.

"Technology traps"

Adaptability problem

So how do we tackle the II evolving and supporting new users in new contexts and the demands that follow?

We can modularize.

"[...] modularity creates a balance between variety and order by localizing the change and permitting fast and deep change in parts of the system. Engineered as well as living systems must thus be modular to remain robust and at the same time to generate variety."

Gateways

Software / component that takes care of communication between different modules in the information infrastructure.

Design principles for the Adaptability problem:

  1. Make each IT capability simple
  2. Modularize the II by building separately its principal functions and sub-infrastructures using layering and gateways
